In the Land of Many Islands

By Pheonix

In the Land of Many Islands

In every rain that flushes streets and rivers,

There pulses anger, the people’s thunder

Because bridges promised crumble,

Because floodwalls betray,

Because contracts shadow moves, and ghosts

Walk through well-lit halls.

They name it “flood control,”

Yet some see rivers stealing lives,

Others see wallets swollen

While concrete wears thin,

While height of typhoon is matched

By depth of betrayal.

And justice is it only in words?

There are trials in The Hague across oceans,

Former presidents summoned for crimes framed

In crusades against drugs, in shadows of silence.

There is an ICC, indictments, reckonings of humanity,

Yet impunity curls in corners.

Names whispered in protests join rosters of grief.

And here at home, hands raised in the streets:

Voices young, old churches, students, mothers, sons

They call: “Where is the account? Where is the apology?”

They demand not just truth, but consequence.

They demand not just walls built, but walls that hold.

Between elections is politics,

And between politics is memory:

Of promises made on stages,

Of dynasties bending states to serve themselves,

Of lives lost, voices silenced,

Of justice delayed perhaps justice denied.

But even in this storm,

Light fractures through clouded sky:

A commission born to investigate,

A Senate inquiry televised,

A citizen breathing wide, refusing to be numb.

For justice is not just law or ledger,

It is memory, it is vigil, it is claim:

That every child’s flood-wrought fear matters,

That every name in a ledger of loss is a human being,

Not just statistic.

So let not fear bury you. Let truth find its echo.

Let accountability not be a chant, but a contract

Between people and power.

Explanation

The poem paints a picture of current tensions in the Philippines where politics, corruption, and justice collide. It refers to the public outrage over corruption in infrastructure projects (like flood control systems) and how these failures harm ordinary people during typhoons and floods. It also touches on the International Criminal Court’s charges against former president Rodrigo Duterte for alleged crimes against humanity in the war on drugs, highlighting the gap between international efforts at accountability and the impunity that still persists at home. The poem shows how people , students, churches, families are demanding transparency, accountability, and real consequences for wrongdoing, not just empty promises. At the same time, the poem suggests that there’s still hope. It mentions investigations, inquiries, and civic movements that push for reform, showing that justice is not only about courts but also about memory, and public pressure. It frames the struggle for justice as a collective act a contract between citizens and power and calls on Filipinos not to be silent or numb but to keep pressing for accountability so that political promises lead to genuine change and protection of lives.
